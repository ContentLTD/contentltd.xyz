On building useful, random things

apr 14, 2026 7 min read journal

There is a particular pleasure in making something that doesn't need to exist.

A few years ago I started keeping a list called useless & useful. The rule was simple — anything I built that solved a problem nobody asked me to solve went on it. A button that played a frog noise. A site that told you what time it was in a single sentence. A tiny app for tracking how many times I'd refilled the same water bottle. None of them changed anything. All of them changed something.

Most of my work happens on weekends, in PA, in a room that gets too warm in the afternoon. I make websites — that's the load-bearing description — but the real work is closer to tending: small communities I keep alive because they're worth keeping alive, scripts I run because nobody else will.

— a workbench, evening

The case for the random

The internet rewards focus. Pick a niche. Build an audience. Compound. I understand the logic and I have, on occasion, tried to follow it — and every time the things I make under that pressure are worse than the things I make in a kind of cheerful drift. The drift is the method. The drift is also the point.

"useless & useful" is not a contradiction — it's the same word said twice, with different stress.

What I've come to believe is that randomness, in moderation, is a form of seriousness. It is the seriousness of refusing to optimise yourself out of the only hours that are actually yours. A Saturday spent building a thing nobody will use is a Saturday you have, in fact, spent.

What this log is for

I'm going to use this place to keep notes — on builds, on books, on the small obsessions that move through. I'll try to be honest about what worked and what didn't. I'll try not to make any of it sound more important than it is. If you found this somehow, I'm glad. Stay as long as you like.
